## Summary

Motorola Solutions announced on June 1 a definitive agreement to acquire D-Fend Solutions, an Israeli company that makes radio-frequency counter-drone systems, for $1.5 billion in cash. The deal adds D-Fend's EnforceAir platform — which identifies a drone's communications protocol, assumes control, and lands it safely — to Motorola's public-safety hardware portfolio. ## Body

Motorola Solutions announced on June 1 that it had signed a definitive agreement to acquire D-Fend Solutions for $1.5 billion in cash. D-Fend, founded in 2016 and headquartered in Ra'anana, Israel, makes EnforceAir: an RF-based system that identifies the communication protocol a drone is using, takes control of the aircraft, and guides it to a safe landing. The platform ships in backpack, vehicle-mounted, and fixed-site configurations. Closing is expected in Q4 2026, subject to regulatory clearance.

Motorola's press release describes thousands of deployments across more than 30 countries, with customers including government agencies, public safety departments, military users, airports, stadiums, and border-security operators. Headcount is contested in the Israeli press: Globes reported approximately 250 employees will move to Motorola as part of the deal, while Ynet cited a figure closer to 200; neither figure appears in the Motorola press release, and the discrepancy is unresolved. Motorola projects D-Fend's 2026 revenue at roughly $185 million and cites more than 50 percent annual revenue growth over the prior three years. Both figures originate from the Motorola press release and D-Fend's own disclosures; they have not been independently audited.

D-Fend has raised approximately $67 million to date. Israeli business press reported divergent figures for its most recent valuation round: Ynet cited roughly $250 million; Globes reported $128 million pre-money from the same transaction. The discrepancy is unresolved — Motorola's announcement supplies no prior valuation — and the $1.5 billion acquisition price represents a substantial step-up from whichever baseline is accurate.

---

**The regulatory hook**

The Safer Skies Act, enacted as part of the FY2026 National Defense Authorization Act, is the regulatory argument Motorola is using to frame the addressable market. The law authorizes trained and certified state and local law enforcement to actively mitigate drone threats — which encompasses the RF takeover approach EnforceAir uses. Until now, active drone interdiction in the United States has been the domain of federal agencies. The Safer Skies Act opening, if implemented at scale, would push counter-drone spending into municipal and county budgets alongside Motorola's existing radio and body-camera business.

Motorola framed the acquisition explicitly in those terms. Whether the law's implementation rules, training requirements, and liability frameworks translate to the addressable market Motorola describes is a policy and procurement question the press release does not address.

---

**Deal context**

Israeli business press, including Globes and Calcalist, described the transaction as the largest-ever acquisition of an Israeli defense-technology company. That framing does not appear in Motorola's materials; it reflects the Israeli business media's characterization, not the acquirer's claim.

For Motorola, the acquisition extends a pattern of public-safety hardware expansion. The company's existing portfolio spans land-mobile radio systems, body cameras, and video security platforms. Adding D-Fend moves it into active drone interdiction — a capability that has shifted from exclusively military deployment to use at civilian infrastructure including airports and stadiums.

Globes reported a Super Bowl deployment among D-Fend's reference installations. That detail carries a single-source attribution and is not confirmed by Motorola's press release or by other outlets in the Source Checker's verified bundle.
