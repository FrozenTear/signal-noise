## Summary

The UK government's mandatory digital identity programme — due to take effect for right-to-work checks by 2029 — now faces a credible internal threat: Andy Burnham, Greater Manchester's mayor and candidate in a by-election that could put him on a path to the Labour leadership, has said he opposes it. ## Body

The UK's digital identity scheme — informally called the "BritCard" — was announced by Prime Minister Keir Starmer in September 2025. By 2029, it is intended to be mandatory for right-to-work checks. In October 2025, a petition against it gathered 2.9 million signatures.

Those signatures did not kill it. A potential prime minister might.

Andy Burnham, Greater Manchester's mayor since 2017 and one of the most recognisable figures in the Labour Party, has stated his opposition to the government's digital identity scheme. His position, corroborated independently by STV News and Labour Conference coverage reported in Insider Media, is unambiguous: not now.

On 19 May 2026, Burnham was selected as the Labour candidate for the Makerfield by-election, scheduled for 18 June. The constituency became available when Josh Simons resigned his Commons seat to stand aside for Burnham. This is a separate act from Simons' earlier departure from a ministerial role — which occurred over a distinct matter involving journalist access — and the two should not be conflated.

To reach the point where Burnham's opposition would determine the BritCard's fate, three sequential conditions must resolve in order: he wins Makerfield on 18 June; he mounts and wins a Labour leadership contest against Starmer or a future successor; and he follows through on his stated position once in office. He is not yet a Member of Parliament. The leadership is not open. And stated positions in opposition do not always survive contact with government.

But the scheme's political fragility was visible well before Burnham filed his candidacy. The 2.9 million-signature petition did not kill the mandatory framework outright — it bent it within months: by January 2026, ministers had already accepted eVisas and e-passports as alternative right-to-work proof, making the scheme functionally optional for those who hold them. The mandatory-first framing that accompanied the September 2025 announcement had quietly retreated by early winter. Burnham's prospective opposition is the second political test of the BritCard's durability. The first came faster than the government planned.

Large-scale identity infrastructure requires years of procurement lead times, vendor contracts, and institutional trust. It does not typically survive a fundamental reversal by the government that commissioned it. The UK is watching, in real time, whether the political coalition behind the BritCard is durable enough to outlast a leadership transition it has not yet had to face.
