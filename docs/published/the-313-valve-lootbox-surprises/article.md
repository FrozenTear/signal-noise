# "Have You Considered Enjoying It?" — Valve Files to Dismiss New York Lootbox Lawsuit

## Summary

Valve has asked a New York court to throw out the state attorney general's lawsuit over Counter-Strike 2's lootbox mechanics. The company's 42-page defense argument comes down to one sentence: people enjoy surprises. The case is pending before NY Supreme Court Justice Nancy Bannon, and whatever it decides will travel to every randomized mechanic on Steam — which means it travels to Linux gaming.

## Body

New York Attorney General Letitia James filed suit in February 2026, alleging that Counter-Strike 2's lootbox cases function as unregulated gambling. Real money goes in. Random outcomes come out. Items trade on secondary markets. The AG's theory, broadly, is that this is gambling under New York law.

Valve, represented by Milbank LLP, filed a 42-page motion to dismiss before NY Supreme Court Justice Nancy Bannon. The core of the defense occupies three sentences:

> "People enjoy surprises. Part of the appeal of many popular collectibles, from baseball cards to cereal box prizes, is the possibility of opening a sealed package and being surprised with a rare item. And while there is an element of randomness in opening a baseball card pack that includes a one-of-a-kind Aaron Judge rookie card, no legislature or court has ever deemed that act illegal gambling."

This is from the filing. It is not a paraphrase.

Valve extends the argument across the landscape of sealed-package consumerism: Pokémon grab bags, comic-book grab bags, cereal-box toys, Happy Meal prizes, LEGO minifig blind bags. The position is that randomized reward mechanics are a feature with established commercial precedent, that courts and legislatures have consistently left this category alone, and that the New York attorney general is asking the court to break with that consensus.

The AG's office is asking the court to do the opposite. The suit holds that CS2 cases — where players spend real money for a randomized chance at a cosmetic item they may or may not want — constitute unregulated gambling because the mechanic is functionally indistinguishable from pulling a slot machine handle. Secondary market value, the theory goes, is what makes it gambling rather than just a bad purchase.

Valve doesn't dispute the mechanics. It disputes the legal category.

For Linux users, the stakes are structural. Steam is the primary gaming platform on Linux — effectively the only major commercial storefront that treats the OS as a first-class target. The Steam Deck runs SteamOS. Whatever the court decides about CS2 cases will travel up the stack to every randomized monetization mechanic on Steam, which is to say: to Linux gaming as a category. A ruling that lootboxes are illegal gambling in New York would not be limited to one title.

The motion to dismiss is Valve arguing the case shouldn't proceed at all. Justice Bannon has yet to rule.
