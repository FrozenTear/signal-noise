## Summary

Public records reviewed by Prism show the network's bulletins from 2020 through 2025 documented protests and traffic delays, not terrorism. ## Body

The Seattle Police Department has operated "Seattle Shield" since 2009. The program is a bidirectional intelligence-sharing arrangement: private businesses and government agencies file Suspicious Activity Reports with SPD, and SPD distributes bulletins, alerts, photographs, and protest updates back to program members. Its stated mission is the identification, deterrence, and defeat of potential acts of terrorism.

The network's membership, documented through FOIA records filed by Seattle privacy activist Phil Mocek since 2012 and exposed in the 2020 BlueLeaks data breach, includes Amazon, Facebook, real estate management firms, Pike Place Market, Seattle Theatre Group, the Downtown Seattle Association, and ICE alongside the FBI's Seattle field office, DHS, the Washington State Fusion Center, NYPD, Nassau County Police, Virginia State Police, Hennepin County Sheriff's Office, and a United Nations threat analyst. The program is described internally as "unfunded" and is managed by SPD Officer Erin Nicholson. The Seattle Shield application was developed and is maintained at no charge by ABM Security Services, which is seeking to offset maintenance costs through a Department of Homeland Security grant.

Seattle Theatre Group confirmed its participation to Prism: "Our operations team works with the Seattle Shield network to receive information that can help keep our venues and audiences safe."

Seattle Shield was modeled on NYPD Shield, established in 2005 in the aftermath of September 11, and operates under the umbrella of the Global Shield Network — a national franchise of public-private intelligence-sharing programs. The 2025 Global Shield Network conference was hosted in Seattle; some attendee photographs in program records were redacted, with SPD citing undercover police operations.

An investigation by Prism Reports, reported by Glen Stellmacher, reviewed Seattle Shield bulletins from 2020 through 2025. According to that review, in 2025 the reports were almost exclusively about protests and potential traffic delays caused by protests throughout Seattle. Specific examples cited by Prism include an October 2025 bulletin issued around the anniversary of Hamas's October 7, 2023 attacks that documented graffiti and property damage at a local tech executive's residence alongside warnings about Palestinian solidarity protests; a June 2025 bulletin characterizing immigration demonstrations at the Federal Building as a security concern; and, during the 2020 Black Lives Matter protests, alerts asking members to retain video surveillance footage and identify potential projectiles.

No terrorism arrest has been publicly attributed to Seattle Shield. In response to Prism's reporting, SPD did not respond to questions about data storage, data retention, technology management, and program oversight. The ACLU of Washington told Prism it had not been monitoring the network.

Former FBI agent and whistleblower Terry Albury, speaking to Prism, described the program's logic: "What did Hoover say? 'I want everyone to believe that there is an FBI agent hiding behind every mailbox.' That's what this list is doing. It's creating the panopticon." Albury characterized the private-sector members as functioning as official informants, formally associated with the intelligence network.

The program's current operating context includes a relevant federal directive. On September 25, 2025, President Trump signed National Security Presidential Memorandum-7 (NSPM-7), titled "Countering Domestic Terrorism and Organized Political Violence." The document identifies "common recurrent motivations and indicia" of domestic terrorism — including anti-capitalism, anti-American views, and hostility to "traditional American views on family, religion, and morality" — as potential markers for preemptive law enforcement focus. Mocek told Prism that the memorandum labels protest activity and protected speech as potential terrorism "indicia." Signal Noise independently confirmed NSPM-7 exists and contains the ideological-marker language Mocek describes; whether the memorandum has materially changed how Seattle Shield bulletins are classified or processed is not established by the available record.

Mocek, who has filed a series of public-records requests on Seattle Shield since 2012, stated to Prism: "Somebody could show up to protest ICE, and then that information gets reported out to Seattle Shield and suddenly they could be on a terrorist watch list? That is not OK."

Seattle Shield's existence is independently corroborated. MuckRock hosts FOIA records under the tag `seattle-shield-program` including a membership roster from June 2013 and membership application records predating this investigation by more than a decade. The 2020 BlueLeaks breach — a widely-reported dataset obtained from a law enforcement web-hosting provider — exposed member lists, IP addresses, and contact information for Seattle Shield. These documents independently establish the network's existence and its public-private membership structure.

What they do not establish — and what SPD did not address in response to Prism's reporting — is how data collected across sixteen years has been stored, what retention policies govern it, who exercises oversight, and whether the program has ever served the purpose it was built to serve.
