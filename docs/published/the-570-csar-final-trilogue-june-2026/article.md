## Summary

The European Parliament's action in late March 2026 ended the temporary ePrivacy derogation that had let platforms voluntarily scan private messages for child sexual abuse material. The mandatory version — the EU's Child Sexual Abuse Regulation, known among its critics as Chat Control 2.0 — never came close to dying. Its final scheduled trilogue round is set for 29 June 2026, with a political deal targeted for July.

---

## Body

In late March 2026, the European Parliament acted on an extension of Regulation 2021/1232 — the temporary ePrivacy derogation, in force since 2021, that had permitted platforms including Meta, Google, and Microsoft to voluntarily scan private messages for child sexual abuse material. The derogation expired in early April.

The outcome drew headlines describing mass surveillance stopped, encryption saved, Chat Control dead.

The instrument those headlines were describing is not what is heading into its decisive round in June.

The Child Sexual Abuse Regulation — known by its acronym CSAR, or "Chat Control 2.0" among its critics — is the proposed permanent framework that would replace the expired derogation. Not merely extending voluntary scanning, but establishing mandatory detection and reporting obligations for all digital platforms, including those using end-to-end encryption. CSAR is alive. It is in active trilogue between the EU Council and the European Parliament. Its final scheduled round is **29 June 2026**.

A political deal is targeted for July.

---

**What CSAR is**

Commissioner Ylva Johansson proposed the Child Sexual Abuse Regulation on 11 May 2022. In its original form, the Commission proposal required platforms to scan the private communications of all users — including those protected by end-to-end encryption — using a combination of hash-matching for known child sexual abuse material and AI-assisted scanning for content not yet in any database.

The European Data Protection Supervisor warned the proposal could enable "de facto generalized and indiscriminate scanning of the content of virtually all types of electronic communications." The European Court of Human Rights, in a February 2024 ruling (*Podchasov v Russia*), held that requiring degraded end-to-end encryption "cannot be regarded as necessary in a democratic society." That ruling is not directly binding on EU institutions — it concerns a Strasbourg court party — but Parliament-side negotiators have cited it extensively as a minimum floor for what can be agreed.

The Commission's proposal failed to advance under the Belgian, Hungarian, and Polish Council presidencies. The Danish presidency, which assumed the rotating chair in July 2025, revised the Council's negotiating position and brought a general approach to trilogue in late 2025.

---

**What the Council conceded — and what it kept**

The Danish presidency's November 2025 position was a substantial retreat from the Commission's original proposal. Explicit "detection orders" — the mechanism that would have compelled platforms to scan all private communications — were dropped.

In their place, the Council substituted a risk-assessment framework. Platforms would be required to evaluate how their services could be misused for child sexual abuse material distribution and implement "all appropriate risk mitigation measures" from a codified list. That list includes voluntary scanning.

The Global Encryption Coalition, which represents major encryption technology stakeholders, described the Council's shift as "a win for encryption" in a January 2026 statement, noting that providers "will no longer be forced to undermine the strong encryption that they offer their customers." The GEC flagged a residual structural concern: the protections apply only to encrypted communications, leaving unencrypted private messages potentially subject to permanent automated scanning under the framework. People who do not know or have not chosen the encryption status of their messaging service would have no equivalent protection.

Former MEP Patrick Breyer, the Pirate Party politician who has publicly led opposition to CSAR since its proposal, characterises the "risk mitigation" language as a back door. Requiring platforms to implement all appropriate measures from a list that includes scanning, he has argued, creates regulatory pressure without a formal mandate — the effect being to normalise mass surveillance of private messages while keeping the legal language at arm's length from direct compulsion. Breyer is an advocacy source whose position is noted as such.

The Council text also includes a review clause. Within three years of the regulation entering into force, the Commission would be required to assess "the necessity and feasibility of including detection obligations." If that assessment is positive, mandatory scanning could return through ordinary legislative procedure.

---

**What Parliament has held**

The European Parliament's November 2023 negotiating mandate — adopted by the LIBE civil liberties committee — drew clear boundaries: no indiscriminate scanning; end-to-end encrypted communications fully excluded from detection obligations; judicial authorization required for any targeted surveillance measure; mandatory age verification rejected.

Parliament's position limits CSAM detection in E2EE environments to metadata analysis: identifying behavioural patterns without accessing message content. Detection of known CSAM via hash-matching on unencrypted platforms is accepted. Detection of previously unknown material, or any content-scanning that touches encrypted communications, is not.

---

**Age verification: the second front**

Separate from the detection question, both the Council and Commission have maintained that platforms offering communication services would be required to implement age verification. Parliament's mandate rejects this.

Privacy advocates argue mandatory age verification is structurally incompatible with anonymous communication: any system that reliably gates access by age requires collecting proof of identity. Tuta, the encrypted email provider, has described mandatory age verification as something that "will harm your right to privacy and kill anonymity online." The concern is not merely about minors — it is about whether anonymous communication survives in a framework where proof of who you are becomes a condition of access.

---

**The technical dispute**

The Internet Watch Foundation published a paper in October 2025 arguing that CSAM can be blocked in E2EE services through "upload prevention" — hash comparisons performed before content is encrypted and sent, without accessing message content. The IWF framed this as evidence that child protection and strong encryption can coexist, directly contesting the premise that E2EE requires a choice between privacy and safety.

Critics of the approach note three limitations: hash-matching identifies only known material, not novel CSAM the Commission's original "unknown content" detection mandate was designed to address; any on-device scanning layer creates infrastructure that could be repurposed or expanded through future legal obligation; and the IWF's paper addresses the technical question but does not resolve the governance question of who controls the hash database and under what oversight.

---

**What 29 June decides**

The June 29 trilogue is the scheduled final round. If agreement is reached, formal adoption by Parliament and Council is expected in July 2026 under the Cypriot presidency. Implementation would follow.

The open questions at that table are substantive. Whether the "risk mitigation" framework effectively exempts encrypted communications or creates sufficient regulatory pressure to change platform behaviour without a formal detection order. Whether mandatory age verification survives, and in what form. What the review clause says about the pathway back to mandatory scanning — the timeframe, the threshold, the procedure.

If June 29 fails to close the gap, the presidency has options: extending the timeline, returning to a revised mandate, or allowing the process to lapse. There is no automatic CSAR. There is also no permanent framework for child protection in private communications; the voluntary regime that preceded it has already expired.

The legislation is a month from a deal, or from failing one more time. The difference in outcomes is not academic — CSAR, if enacted with mandatory detection provisions intact, would establish the first EU legal basis for systematic surveillance of private encrypted communications. If enacted without them, it would establish a risk-assessment framework whose practical effect on encryption will depend on implementation, enforcement, and whatever the Commission's three-year review concludes.

Both outcomes follow from what happens on 29 June. The moment the headlines about Chat Control's death were celebrating has not arrived.
