## Body

The annual operating budget for the Emergency Events Database — EM-DAT — is €300,000. Approximately 90% of that has come, for more than twenty years, from a single USAID grant to the Centre for Research on the Epidemiology of Disasters (CRED) at the Université catholique de Louvain (UCLouvain) in Belgium. In February 2025, Prof. Niko Speybroeck, EM-DAT's director, received a letter notifying him the grant was ending. By July 2025, USAID had been closed.

What the database contains is not in dispute. Since 1988, EM-DAT has compiled records of more than 27,000 natural and technological disasters — floods, storms, droughts, heatwaves, industrial accidents, transport disasters — drawing on reports from United Nations agencies, national governments, insurers, and humanitarian organisations. The database is free and open-access. It is a primary source for IPCC assessments of disaster frequency and economic loss trends. The African Union has used it for extreme weather reporting since 2018. Indonesia has drawn on it for national disaster strategy development.

"EM-DAT can be considered the world's memory of disasters," Speybroeck told Carbon Brief in May 2026. "It contains more than 27,000 natural and technological disasters."

EM-DAT's commitment to free access is the basis of its scientific value and the source of its financial vulnerability. A database that charges for access can diversify revenue. A database committed to open access depends on institutional funding — and EM-DAT structured that dependence around a single bilateral grant from a US foreign-aid program. That structure held for over two decades.

---

**The funding collapse**

The Trump administration announced the dismantling of USAID in January 2025, shortly after the second inauguration. The foreign-aid freeze moved quickly through grant portfolios. Speybroeck received his termination notice within weeks.

EM-DAT's 2026 Roadmap, published in January of that year, acknowledged the loss in institutional language: "After more than 20 years of support, the USAID grant that helped sustain EM-DAT's core work has come to an end." The Roadmap outlines a recovery path: commercial subscription plans for higher-volume users, a premium tier with enhanced data access features, and an Advisory Board explicitly tasked with identifying diversified funding.

As of May 2026, Speybroeck reports "positive signals" from potential funders. He is direct about what that means: "there is nothing on paper yet."

The gap between positive signals and a signed, multi-year funding commitment is where the operational risk lives. EM-DAT has not announced a closure date. It has not announced a replacement funder.

---

**Who depends on it**

Prof. Dewald van Niekerk, head of the African Centre for Disaster Studies at North-West University in South Africa, described the practical function of the database: "We use it on various levels, from sub-national straight up to continental level."

Van Niekerk named the inequality dimension that sits beneath the technical one. "Not all [African] governments can compile these databases. Where they do, they are extremely fragmented." For countries without the institutional capacity to maintain independent disaster records, EM-DAT is not supplementary to something else — it is the only consistent longitudinal record available.

Prof. Gabriele Messori, director of the Swedish Centre for Impacts of Climate Extremes at Uppsala University, identified the methodological problem a gap in the record creates: "It's very worrying that a long-term dataset that has become a reference for many different sectors...could be suddenly interrupted."

Interrupted is the operative word. EM-DAT's value to climate science and humanitarian planning is specifically as a continuous, multi-decade series beginning in 1988. A break in 2026 would not merely end a data service — it would permanently mark the record. Trend analysis comparing post-2026 disaster frequency and loss data against the prior 38-year run would carry an unresolvable methodological caveat: the source changed, the methodology may have changed, and continuity cannot be assumed.

---

**The sovereignty dimension**

Speybroeck noted one thing the USAID relationship had provided beyond money: insulation from political pressure. "[USAID] allowed us to work in an independent and neutral way, so we were not influenced by any politics."

That observation carries structural weight. A Belgian university's disaster database, built on a US foreign-aid instrument, maintained its scientific independence partly because the funding relationship was institutionalised enough to provide distance from political interference. The loss of that funding — triggered by a domestic US political decision — is a demonstration of the exposure inherent in that design: a public-good scientific dataset whose survival depends on the foreign-policy continuity of a single government.

An open letter calling for alternative funding from governments, multilateral development banks, and philanthropic institutions was published in March 2026. More than 4,000 academics and researchers signed it. The letter reflects the scientific community's investment in the database. It does not reflect a signed replacement.

---

**What the €300,000 problem actually is**

The budget is almost incidental. €300,000 is a small operating cost for a global scientific infrastructure resource — smaller than many university department budgets. The problem is not that the gap is too large to fill. The problem is structural: filling it requires a new institutional home for the dependency — a government, multilateral body, or philanthropic institution willing to commit recurring, multi-year funding to a Belgian academic centre for a database they do not control and cannot brand.

The EM-DAT 2026 Roadmap's turn toward commercial subscriptions and premium tiers signals that CRED is exploring whether partial self-funding is possible. Whether that path is compatible with maintaining free open access for the researchers, governments, and humanitarian agencies in low-capacity settings — the users for whom EM-DAT is not redundant to anything else — is not resolved by the Roadmap.

Van Niekerk's conclusion on what needs to change is structural: any successor funding arrangement needs to ensure that "no one can just pull the plug."

For 38 years, someone could.
