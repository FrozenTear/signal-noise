## Summary

The federal Take It Down Act's one-year compliance deadline expired on May 19, 2026. The FTC began accepting complaints via a new portal, sent warning letters to 15 major platforms before the deadline and 12 unnamed "nudify" AI sites after it, and filed zero enforcement actions. Platform compliance ranges from a dedicated TIDA help page at Discord to conspicuous silence at X.

## Body

The Tools to Address Known Exploitation by Immobilizing Technological Deepfakes on Websites and Networks Act — TAKE IT DOWN — was signed into law on May 19, 2025. One year later to the day, the law's civil compliance clock ran out.

Section 3 of the Act requires every covered platform to maintain a mechanism through which victims can request the removal of nonconsensual intimate images, real or AI-generated. A valid request starts a 48-hour window: the platform must remove the content and make reasonable efforts to identify and remove known identical copies. Noncompliance is treated as a violation of the Federal Trade Commission Act. Civil penalties reach $53,088 per violation.

The FTC is the enforcement authority. Here is what it did on deadline day.

**The FTC's opening move**

The FTC did not file enforcement actions on May 19. It opened a complaints portal — TakeItDown.ftc.gov — where victims can report platforms that ignored a removal request or never established any removal process at all. The agency simultaneously partnered with the National Center for Missing and Exploited Children on a parallel portal, takeitdown.ncmec.org, for cases involving minors.

Before the deadline, FTC Chairman Andrew N. Ferguson sent warning letters to 15 named companies: Alphabet, Amazon, Apple, Automattic, Bumble, Discord, Match Group, Meta, Microsoft, Pinterest, Reddit, SmugMug, Snapchat, TikTok, and X. The letters reminded them of their obligation to comply no later than May 19. They were reminders, not findings.

After the deadline, the FTC sent a second wave of warning letters — this time to 12 unnamed websites operating AI "nudify" tools that digitally remove clothing from photographs of real people to generate sexualized images. These platforms had failed to provide any removal mechanism at all. The FTC chose not to name them publicly. Chairman Ferguson stated: "Platforms no longer have any excuses — they must comply with their obligations under the TAKE IT DOWN Act or face the consequences."

**The roll call**

Meta maintains a dedicated compliance page covering Facebook, Instagram, and Threads. The page, updated in the week following the compliance deadline, describes a reporting flow through the three-dot menu on posts, or via a standalone form for users without accounts. It directs adults to StopNCII.org — an industry tool that matches flagged images using perceptual hashes without storing them — and directs minors to NCMEC's portal. URL: meta.com/help/policies/1437976901029950.

Discord published a help article titled specifically "How to Report Non-Consensual Intimate Imagery Under The Take It Down Act," directing U.S. users to a web form at discord.com/report. The article appears at support.discord.com/hc/en-us/articles/38675715591831, confirmed in search results from May 2026. The page itself returned 403 on direct fetch on May 29, 2026.

TikTok, Reddit, and Snap are members of StopNCII.org. Digital Trends, reviewing the compliance landscape on May 19, reported that TikTok provides a dedicated removal form and that Reddit has a visible removal process. Neither platform's compliance page was directly accessible for independent verification during the preparation of this article.

X has an existing non-consensual nudity policy page at help.x.com/en/rules-and-policies/intimate-media, confirmed via search results. The page returned 403 on direct fetch on May 29. No TIDA-specific compliance article appeared in search results for X. Digital Trends, citing Wired reporting, noted that when press outlets contacted platforms about TIDA compliance, X did not respond.

**The gap between a page and a process**

The Act does not require transparency reporting. Platforms are obligated to post a removal mechanism — they are not obligated to publish how many requests they received, how many they honored, or whether the 48-hour window held. Riana Pfefferkorn, writing at Techdirt on the deadline day, observed this gap and called on platforms to voluntarily add TIDA data to their annual transparency reports by end of 2026.

What independent tracking exists is not encouraging on effectiveness. Research reviewed by TechPolicy.Press found that where platforms established nominal takedown procedures, observed removal volumes were low. Researchers found that the supply and demand for the category of content TIDA targets "sharply increased after the TAKE IT DOWN Act's passage." The law's first year appears not to have functioned as a deterrent.

**The EFF's objections remain**

The Electronic Frontier Foundation opposed the Act from its introduction and has not updated that assessment since enforcement began. Jason Kelley, writing for the EFF in April 2025, characterized the law as providing "the powerful a dangerous new route to manipulate platforms into removing lawful speech that they simply don't like." Joe Mullin's February 2025 analysis flagged a technical concern that the deadline's passage has not resolved: the 48-hour removal window may give encrypted platforms a compliance incentive to abandon encryption rather than risk liability for content they cannot inspect.

That concern intersects with a documented fact. Meta removed end-to-end encryption from Instagram's direct messages on May 8, 2026 — eleven days before TIDA's compliance deadline. Meta's stated reason was low adoption; the company did not publicly connect the removal to TIDA.

**The ledger**

FTC enforcement actions filed as of May 29, 2026: zero.

Major platforms with a confirmed, dedicated removal process: Meta, Discord.

Major platforms with removal processes confirmed via secondary reporting and StopNCII membership: TikTok, Reddit, Snap.

Major platforms whose TIDA-specific compliance posture is unclear: X.

Unnamed companies warned post-deadline for having no removal process: 12.

The FTC has authority to pursue civil penalties of $53,088 per violation. It has not used that authority yet.
