## Body

On December 31, 2025, the Hellenic Data Protection Authority (HDPA) issued Decision 45/2025. It found that Greece's "Smart Policing" biometric surveillance programme would constitute unlawful personal data processing if deployed. This was not a finding about misuse. The system had not been misused. It had not been used at all.

Greece's Ministry of Citizen Protection contracted Intracom Telecom in 2019 to build and supply 1,000 portable surveillance devices for the Hellenic Police. Each unit combined facial recognition, fingerprint identification, document scanning, and vehicle licence plate reading. Officers in the field could query SIS II, VIS, EURODAC, Europol's databases, and third-country records in real time from the device. The total contract value was €4 million — 75 percent drawn from the EU Internal Security Fund, the remaining 25 percent from Greek state funds. The devices were manufactured and delivered to police in September 2021.

They were never activated.

The reason begins in December 2019, two years before delivery, when civil society organisations Homo Digitalis and AlgorithmWatch published a joint investigation exposing the programme. In March 2020, Homo Digitalis — an EDRi member organisation — filed a formal complaint with the HDPA. In August 2020, the authority opened an official investigation. Devices arrived in September 2021 while that investigation was still open. The HDPA's ruling, when it came four years later, confirmed what the complaint had alleged.

Decision 45/2025 found two violations, each independently sufficient to establish unlawful processing.

The first: no valid legal basis. Greek and EU law require that any processing of personal data — and real-time facial recognition of individuals in public space constitutes biometric data processing — rest on a specific, documented legal foundation. The "Smart Policing" programme had none. Greece did not establish the legislative basis that would authorise this class of field-level biometric identification before procuring the hardware to do it.

The second: no timely Data Protection Impact Assessment. GDPR and its law enforcement analogue, the Law Enforcement Directive, require high-risk data processing systems to undergo a formal impact assessment before deployment. For "Smart Policing," no DPIA was conducted at the required stage. The HDPA characterised this as an original design flaw, not a correctable administrative gap.

Homo Digitalis described the combined finding plainly: the system was "unlawful by design." It did not become noncompliant through operation. The design itself was the problem.

The devices were paid for. The money was transferred. The hardware was delivered and is, as of this writing, held by the Hellenic Police. What the HDPA's ruling means in practice is that the authority warned the police not to activate the system. There is no reported disposal order. There is no reported appeal of Decision 45/2025 as of May 2026.

The HDPA is not an outlier for finding this kind of procurement unlawful. Across the EU, data protection authorities have repeatedly ruled — after the fact — that surveillance infrastructure was procured and contracted without the legal framework to operate it. The EU AI Act, which entered into force in August 2024, designates real-time remote biometric identification in public spaces as high-risk AI, subject to additional restrictions in law enforcement use cases. Whether "Smart Policing" would have met AI Act requirements is a question that may not require an answer: the system failed on GDPR and the Law Enforcement Directive before the AI Act was written.

The timeline spans six years. The 2019 contract. The 2019 investigation. The 2020 complaint. The 2021 delivery. The 2025 ruling. At each step after the contract was signed, the legal path to lawful activation narrowed. By the time the HDPA issued its decision, it had already been five years since civil society had publicly argued the system was unlawful. The authority agreed.

Four million euros. One thousand devices. Zero activations.
