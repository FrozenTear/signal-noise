## Summary

Britain's Starmer government has announced a national digital ID scheme — but as of now there is no law, just a live public consultation. Here is what is actually being proposed, why the "mandatory" question keeps shifting onto employers rather than individuals, and what civil-liberties groups are warning about.

## Body

Britain is getting a digital ID. Or maybe it isn't. Or maybe it will, but only if you want to work legally, and even then only from 2029, and even then only if your employer doesn't just accept your biometric passport instead.

This is the peculiar state of the Starmer government's national digital identity scheme — a proposal announced with fanfare in September 2025, now sitting in public consultation, already reshaped once by political pressure, and carrying a civil-liberties opposition petition that reached roughly 2.9 million signatures before anyone had voted on it.

The details matter here, because this story has been misreported more than almost any policy area in tech. Let's do it properly.

## What is actually being proposed

On **26 September 2025**, the government announced a free digital ID scheme that would let UK citizens and legal residents carry a government-verified identity credential on their smartphone, stored in a GOV.UK digital wallet.

According to the official gov.uk explainer — the closest thing to a primary source outside enacted legislation — the credential would hold: your name, date of birth, nationality or residency status, and photograph. Whether it would also include your home address is one of the things the current consultation, published in **March 2026**, has not yet resolved.

The wallet would not replace physical documents. Passports, Irish passport cards, and eVisas would remain valid for most purposes. The government's own framing is as a convenience and fraud-reduction tool, not a compulsory carry document.

## The "mandatory" question — and why it keeps shifting

Here is the nuance that every headline seems to either overstate or understate.

When the scheme was announced in September 2025, the government's position was that digital ID would be mandatory to prove the right to work in the UK. That framing generated the petition, the backlash, and substantial parliamentary pressure. By **January 2026**, the government had softened: digital ID would still be required for Right-to-Work checks by 2029, but it would not be the *only* accepted form. A biometric British or Irish passport, an Irish passport card, or an eVisa would all remain valid alternatives.

The other thing getting lost in most coverage: the legal obligation, such as it is, falls on **employers** to check workers' right to work via the scheme — not on individuals to carry the ID at all times or to register for it. Computer Weekly's analysis puts the design challenge sharply: the scheme "may be neither mandatory nor national" in the way most people assume.

Exemptions are built in. The credential is not required for pensioners, students not in employment, or people accessing healthcare. And non-smartphone alternatives have been promised, though their form remains unspecified in the consultation.

## The stated case for it

The government makes two main arguments: fraud reduction and administrative simplification.

On fraud: digital verification with a government-backed credential is harder to forge than photocopied documents in the right-to-work chain. The current system, where employers photocopy passports and file them in cabinets, is a paper-thin check easily defeated with a quality printer.

On simplification: a portable, phone-based credential could replace a cumbersome stack of document-sharing for rental applications, age verification, professional licensing checks, and employment onboarding. The government has pointed to this as a way to cut cost and friction for both individuals and businesses.

## The civil-liberties objections

The objections are not fringe. Civil-liberties groups — including the Electronic Frontier Foundation (which weighed in internationally) and the privacy organisation Statewatch — alongside parliamentary campaigners, have raised four categories of concern:

**Function creep.** Digital identity schemes have a documented history of scope expansion. A credential introduced for right-to-work checks can become the de facto key to banking, healthcare, housing, and government services, even without explicit legislative mandate. Critics argue the consultation's silence on future permitted uses is itself an answer.

**Exclusion by design.** A phone-based system creates a tiered identity infrastructure where those without smartphones — older, poorer, or more isolated people — face a different documentary burden. The promised non-smartphone alternative has not been specified.

**State surveillance.** A centralised verification log potentially tells the government when and where citizens are asserting their identity. Even a privacy-by-design architecture can be compelled to retain or produce records under existing law. Statewatch's joint parliamentary briefing argued that the petition debate never adequately addressed these structural risks.

**"National" is contested.** Computer Weekly's reading of the technical architecture suggests the scheme may not be a unified national database in the way the term implies — but the political framing invites exactly that interpretation, and the gap between what the government says and what people hear is itself a transparency problem.

## What actually exists right now

This is where the transparency hook lands, and it is significant.

As of the date of this article, **there is no law.** What exists is a policy announcement (September 2025) followed by a public consultation published in **March 2026**. The consultation is still live. Parliament has not passed the legislation that would give the scheme statutory force. The 2029 rollout target is the government's stated ambition, not an enacted date.

The 2.9-million-signature petition, the tabloid alarmism about compulsory ID cards, the op-eds from civil libertarians — all of it is responding to a proposal, not a fait accompli. That does not make the concerns less legitimate. Proposals become law; consultations are routinely used to paper over decisions already made. But readers deserve to know the difference.
