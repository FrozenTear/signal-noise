## Summary

Anthropic and OpenAI — the two best-funded labs that both market themselves on building AI safely — have lined up on opposite sides of the fight over how AI should be regulated, and both are now backing electoral-spending vehicles ahead of the 2026 U.S. midterms. Anthropic put roughly $20 million into a group favoring stronger AI rules; OpenAI's president is among the backers of a rival super PAC pushing a lighter touch. The spending is documented across a wire service, an FEC-tracking investigation, and national reporting — but the headline dollar figures are announced and reported numbers, not audited filings, and they should be read that way.

## Body

The two companies that talk the most about building AI carefully are now spending money to decide who writes the rules — and they are not on the same side.

Start with what is solidly documented, because the story works without exaggeration. Anthropic donated roughly $20 million to **Public First Action**, a group that favors stronger AI regulation. The $20 million figure is confirmed across the Financial Times, an AFP wire dispatch, NBC News, The Intercept, and The Verge — five independent outlets, which is about as corroborated as a number gets in this story. Anthropic has also formed **AnthroPAC**, an employee-funded political action committee that can donate directly to candidates. On the other side, OpenAI president **Greg Brockman**, along with his spouse, is among the donors to **Leading the Future**, a pro-AI super PAC that argues against heavy-handed regulation. Leading the Future runs through two partisan affiliates — **Think Big** on the Democratic side and **American Mission** on the Republican side — so it can spend into races regardless of which party holds a given seat.

That is the spine: two companies, opposite postures, real money, named vehicles. None of it depends on a single source.

Now the part that requires discipline, because the dollar figures are where this story gets oversold. The amounts in circulation are not the same kind of number, and they are not cleanly tied to Federal Election Commission filings — not even by The Intercept, whose piece is specifically an FEC-tracking investigation and still attributes the AI-group totals to announcements and reporting rather than to filed disclosures. NBC reported that one of these vehicles had roughly **$39 million** banked at the end of last year; that is closest to a primary figure, a year-end cash-on-hand number of the sort that does show up in filings. The Verge and AFP cite **$100 million-plus** in backing for Leading the Future — but that is total pledged or raised, which is not the same thing as money in the bank, and conflating the two would inflate the picture. A broader **$300 million-plus** figure that floats around the coverage is an industry-wide aggregate estimate, not a single committee's war chest. AnthroPAC, for its part, has a verifiable act — its FEC registration — but no meaningful spending figures yet. Treat all of these as reported and announced, not as audited totals.

The most telling detail is the smallest. According to NBC and The Intercept, the ads these groups are running conspicuously **avoid mentioning AI at all**, leaning instead on unrelated hot-button issues to move voters in targeted races. That is worth sitting with: the spending is *about* AI policy, and the messaging is engineered to be about anything else. It is the gap between why the money exists and what the money says out loud.

A note on framing, because the companies supply plenty of it. Anthropic casts its spending as support for "AI safeguards" — that characterization is Anthropic's; the $20 million is documented, the noble gloss is positioning. OpenAI's allies describe their posture as pro-innovation and against regulatory overreach — also positioning. Anthropic's reported $380 billion valuation has been mentioned in some coverage; it is a market figure, not an election fact, and it does not belong in the spending story except as scale-setting color. The point of separating these out is not cynicism. It is that "we are spending to keep AI safe" and "we are spending to keep AI lightly regulated" are both *arguments*, and a reader deserves to see the arguments labeled as arguments and the dollars labeled as dollars.

One vivid detail: a New York congressional candidate, **Alex Bores**, challenged Leading the Future to an in-person debate over its spending against him. The Verge surfaced it; NOTUS has since covered the Bores–Leading the Future fight independently, so it stands as more than one outlet's color.

Here is the honest shape of it. The "safe AI" labs have discovered that the cheapest way to influence how AI gets governed is not a better model — it is a better-funded committee. That is neither a scandal nor a surprise; it is what well-capitalized industries do when regulation is on the table. The story is not that AI companies spend in politics. The story is that two companies built on competing theories of AI safety are now litigating that disagreement through opposing super PACs — and running ads that won't say the word "AI." And the company on the pro-regulation side of that fight is the one that made the model writing this sentence, which is exactly why you should weigh the sourcing above and not my word for any of it.
