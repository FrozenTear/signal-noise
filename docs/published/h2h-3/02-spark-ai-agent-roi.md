## Summary

Vendors sold every CFO an agent army in 2025. In 2026 the audits arrived. MIT's primary research puts 95% of GenAI pilots at zero measurable P&L impact after $30–40B spent. Three public pullbacks and three real deployments show exactly where the line sits — and why the gap between pitch decks and CFO reality keeps widening.

## Body

### The 95% Problem

MIT's August 2025 *The GenAI Divide* report found 95% of enterprise GenAI pilots deliver zero P&L impact. Vendor-bought solutions succeed at double the rate of internally built ones (67% vs ~33%). Back-office automation outperforms the sales and marketing projects that still consume most budgets.

Gartner's June 2025 release projects the bill: over 40% of agentic AI projects will be canceled by end of 2027. Only 19% of enterprises have made significant investments.

The [METR randomized trial](https://metr.org) delivered the sharpest data point on the perception gap. Sixteen experienced developers were 19% slower with AI coding assistants while believing they were 20% faster. Vendor "55% faster" claims do not survive controlled measurement.

### Three Pullbacks on the Record

**Klarna** reversed its celebrated AI customer-service replacement. The company once claimed the system handled two-thirds of interactions and replaced ~700 agents. In May 2025 CEO Sebastian Siemiatkowski admitted quality had dropped and the firm was rehiring humans. The reversal is on the record.

**McDonald's and IBM** killed their drive-thru "Automated Order Taker" test in July 2024 after 100+ restaurants. The system never hit 95% accuracy and did not save franchisees money. Failures included ordering for adjacent lanes and suggesting "bacon on ice cream." The pilot was turned off.

**New York City's MyCity chatbot** cost ~$600K and told business owners they could legally pocket workers' tips and refuse Section 8 vouchers — both illegal. The city shut it down.

These are CEO admissions, regulatory records, and municipal budget lines.

### Three Deployments That Scaled (With Receipts)

**GitHub Copilot** is the clearest production win. GitHub reports 4.7M paid subscribers (Jan 2026), 90% of Fortune 100 adoption, and ~46% of code in active sessions generated by the tool. The honest counterweight is the METR trial: the perception gap is real.

**JPMorgan** has publicly committed to agentic automation across investment-banking workflows — COiN for M&A memos, trade settlement, fraud. Claims of replacing 20+ analyst hours per week appear in coverage. The headline "450+ use cases" currently traces more to press than a single audited filing.

**Walmart** runs autonomous replenishment agents across thousands of stores without per-decision human approval. One circulated "22% e-commerce lift" figure traces to a single industry blog and is not corroborated here. The defensible fact is the live, large-scale production deployment.

### Hidden Costs and the Four-Test Bar

Sprawl is measurable: 96% of enterprises run AI agents, 94% cite complexity or governance concerns, only 12% have central management. Eval overhead runs $0.01–$0.10 per sample; serious agents need 100+ iterations. API and hosting costs per agent range from hundreds to tens of thousands monthly before oversight headcount.

For complex knowledge work the fully loaded agentic cost can exceed the human equivalent once eval, correction, and maintenance are counted.

The working definition of good ROI that survives the data is a four-test bar:

1. Measurable P&L impact, not headcount theater. (95% fail here per MIT.)
2. Bought, not built, for back-office automation. (67% success vs ~33%.)
3. Explicit success criteria set up front.
4. Net of oversight, eval, and compute overhead — not gross.

Anything that cannot clear all four remains on the hype side of the divide.

### Category Reality Check

Coding assistants are visible but contested. Customer support saw the loudest reversal; Klarna is now the reference case against full-replacement narratives. Back-office automation is the quiet outperformer in the primary data.

The 2026 story is not that agents don't work. It is that the universal easy wins were oversold, the durable ones require discipline most pilots skipped, and the four tests above are now the minimum price of admission.
