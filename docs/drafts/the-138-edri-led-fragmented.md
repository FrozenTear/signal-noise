# The GDPR Has a Sibling for the Police. Years On, It Still Isn't Working the Same Way Twice.

**Summary.** There is a second EU data-protection law — one written specifically for police and prosecutors, not for advertisers or app makers. The digital-rights group EDRi published a study on 20 May 2026 finding that this law, the Law Enforcement Directive, is still applied inconsistently across the member states it examined. The finding lands inside a live window: under the directive's own review clause (Article 62), the European Commission's mandated evaluation of the same directive is due in May 2026.

## Body

Most people who have heard of European data-protection law have heard of one law: the GDPR, the rulebook that produced the cookie banners and the "we value your privacy" pop-ups. Fewer have heard of its sibling.

It is called the Law Enforcement Directive — Directive (EU) 2016/680, passed alongside the GDPR in 2016. It governs a different and arguably more sensitive category of data processing: what police forces, prosecutors, and other criminal-justice authorities are allowed to do with personal data. Suspect records, victim and witness data, biometric matches, watchlists. The GDPR, broadly, does not cover that activity; the LED does. EDRi describes it as the GDPR's "little sister," and the description is apt — same family, far less attention.

On 20 May 2026, EDRi — European Digital Rights, a Brussels-based network of digital-civil-liberties organisations — published an evaluation of how that directive has actually been implemented. Its conclusion, in EDRi's own words, is that implementation remains "fragmented and insufficient." The "insufficient" is EDRi's normative judgement, and worth flagging as such; the "fragmented" is the more concrete, checkable claim, and it is the one the study is built to support.

The study, a Shadow Evaluation Report, examined five member states: Bulgaria, France, Germany, Greece, and Slovenia. It looked at four areas of the directive:

- **The rights the directive gives ordinary people** over police-held data — the rights to information, access, rectification, and erasure set out in Articles 13 to 17.
- **Special-category, or "sensitive," data** — Article 10 — covering things like biometric and genetic data, ethnicity, political opinions, or health, where the directive sets a higher bar.
- **How well national law lines up** with the directive's own framework — Article 8.
- **New technologies and big-data policing**, where the volume and automation of processing strain rules written for an earlier era.

Across those areas, EDRi reports that the five states have not landed in the same place. The directive sets a floor — minimum standards every member state is supposed to meet — but a directive, unlike a regulation, is transposed into national law country by country. That design leaves room for divergence, and EDRi's finding is that the room has been used: rights that are real in one country are weaker or harder to exercise in another.

That structural problem is not only EDRi's claim. A separate, independent assessment for the European Parliament — produced for its policy department in December 2022 by researchers Olga Vogiatzoglou and Thomas Marquenie — documented shortcomings in how member states had transposed the directive and divergent interpretations of its requirements. The EDRi study is fresh and its framing is advocacy; the underlying problem of uneven national implementation has been on the record from the EU's own institutions for years.

Why it matters for someone who is not a privacy lawyer: the LED is the law that is supposed to let you find out what the police hold on you, correct it if it is wrong, and have it deleted when it should not be there. If the directive is implemented unevenly, those guarantees are not the same depending on which member state's authority holds your data — and the protections weaken precisely in the part of public life, criminal justice, where the stakes for getting it wrong are highest.

The timing is not incidental. The directive's own review clause — Article 62 — required the European Commission to evaluate the LED by 6 May 2022 and every four years thereafter, which puts the next mandated review in May 2026. EDRi's report drops into that evaluation window — a civil-society shadow report arriving alongside the official review, which is a common and deliberate pattern in EU policy advocacy. Separately, the European Data Protection Board adopted a report in early 2026 to support the Commission's evaluation, confirming the official process was underway. EDRi is, in effect, putting its own evidence on the table while the EU grades its own homework.

One limit worth stating plainly: as of this writing, no independent newsroom has re-reported EDRi's specific study. The report is roughly a day old. The factual spine — that the study exists, examined those five states and four areas, and found inconsistent implementation — is solid and corroborated on the underlying problem. The verdict that this is "insufficient" belongs to EDRi.
