### Body

The headline number cleared. The line items didn't.

Home Depot's Q1 FY2026 print, released 2026-05-19, posted sales of $41.77B against a $41.52B consensus — a +4.8% YoY beat of roughly 60 basis points on the top line. Adjusted diluted EPS of $3.43 cleared the $3.41 estimate by two cents. By the time the market opened, the stock was off 2.5% pre-market. The dispersion between the headline and the tape is the story.

Start with positioning. In the seven days before the print, Wells Fargo cut its price target from $420 to $375 (2026-05-14) and Truist cut its from $424 to $394 (2026-05-13). Those are valuation targets, not the revenue or EPS bar — but they tell you sell-side walked into the quarter bearish. A ~60 bps top-line beat and a two-cent EPS clear, against that backdrop, reads as a modest beat into a cautious setup, not the upside surprise the headline font implies.

Below the headline, GAAP earnings tell the other half. GAAP net earnings came in at $3.3B, down from $3.4B a year ago, and GAAP diluted EPS fell to $3.30 from $3.45 — a YoY decline on the cleanest accounting line in the release. The wedge between adjusted EPS up and GAAP EPS down is the kind of thing margin-watchers price immediately and management talks past.

The margin line is where the deterioration shows up structurally. Gross margin compressed approximately 75 bps to 33%. On a $41.77B revenue base, 75 bps of gross margin is roughly $313M of foregone gross profit (derived: 0.0075 × $41.77B) — meaningful against a quarterly GAAP net of $3.3B. The company's reaffirmation of its FY26 guide — sales +2.5% to +4.5%, comps ~flat to +2.0%, adjusted EPS growth ~flat to +4.0% — is doing the work of holding the multiple intact while the gross-margin trajectory does not.

Composition of the top line matters more than the top line itself. U.S. comp sales came in at +0.4%, total comps at +0.6%, against a Street whisper closer to +0.9%. Transactions fell -1.3%; average ticket rose to $92.76 from $90.71. The arithmetic is straightforward: comps are positive because customers are spending more per visit, not because more customers are visiting. In a category whose unit economics depend on repeat traffic — paint, hardware, seasonal — a ticket-led comp is a different beast from a traffic-led one.

CFO Richard McPhail named the mechanism on the call: homeowners "will defer their spend on larger projects." That is not a forward-looking concern. That is a CFO confirming, on the record, the through-line that has been visible in the transaction count for several quarters. The deferral thesis — that mortgage rates anchored well above 6% have frozen the existing-home turnover that historically pulls big-ticket renovation forward — now has a management quote attached. CEO Ted Decker's framing that the company remains "bullish on the long-term … prospects in US Housing" is the bull case for FY27+, not for the current cycle.

The pieces a margin-aware reader weights: a modest beat (~60 bps top line, two cents on EPS) into sell-side that was cutting price targets the week before; GAAP EPS down YoY; 75 bps of gross-margin compression; comps that missed the whisper; transactions contracting while ticket grew; and a CFO confirming big-ticket deferral on the record. The pieces a category bull weights: top-line beat, ticket up, guide reaffirmed.

The market's verdict at -2.5% pre-market is which line the price discovered. One quarter of margin compression is one quarter, not a trend — that caveat is load-bearing. But it does make the reaffirmed FY26 guide the load-bearing element of the bull case. If sales come in at the low end of +2.5% and the gross-margin trajectory holds, the adjusted EPS growth band — flat to +4.0% — does not. That is the line to watch into Q2.
