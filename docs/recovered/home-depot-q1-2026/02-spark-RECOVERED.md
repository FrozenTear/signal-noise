# H2H-B1 Draft — Spark

**Title:** Home Depot's CFO Just Handed the Bears Their Confirmation

**Byline:** Dax Okafor — Reporter, Business & Finance (Spark / Grok 4.3)

**Summary:** Home Depot cleared a lowered bar on revenue and adjusted earnings, then reaffirmed its full-year guide. The detail worth watching came from CFO Richard McPhail: homeowners "will defer their spend on larger projects." The market heard that part loudest.

## Body

Home Depot's Q1 print is the kind of release where the headline and the subtext are written for different audiences.

The company posted sales of $41.77B, up 4.8% year over year and above the $41.52B consensus. Adjusted diluted EPS came in at $3.43 versus the $3.41 estimate. Management then did what managements do when they want the story to stay intact: they reaffirmed the fiscal 2026 guide — sales growth 2.5% to 4.5%, comps flat to up 2.0%, adjusted EPS growth flat to up 4.0%.

The stock still fell roughly 2.5% pre-market.

The reason sits in the transcript and the 8-K. GAAP net earnings dropped to $3.3B from $3.4B. GAAP diluted EPS fell to $3.30 from $3.45. Gross margin compressed about 75 basis points to 33%. Customer transactions declined 1.3%. U.S. comparable sales were only +0.4%; total comps +0.6%, below the roughly 0.9% whisper the Street had settled on after two pre-print price target cuts.

Average ticket rose to $92.76 from $90.71. The Pro segment held up. The company took share. Those are the lines management and long-only bulls will cite.

The CFO's comment cuts across them. Homeowners, he said, "will defer their spend on larger projects." CEO Ted Decker stayed bullish on the long-term housing outlook. The two statements are not in direct contradiction, but they are aimed at different time horizons and different stakeholders.

The beat was real against a bar that had already been marked down — Wells Fargo to $375, Truist to $394 — in the week before the print. The guide reaffirmation is real in the narrow sense that management is not lowering the range it gave in January. What the data shows underneath is a consumer who is still buying, but is skipping the high-margin, big-ticket work that used to drive both the top line and the margin profile.

That is the split the H2H is built on. One camp reads the reaffirmed numbers and the Pro strength and sees a franchise that can compound through a soft cycle. The other camp reads the transaction decline, the margin compression, the explicit deferral language, and the whisper miss, and sees management leaning on a guide that assumes the deferred spending eventually shows up on the same trajectory they laid out when the data was better.

The stock reaction suggests which framing landed with the people who move the price. Analyst notes into the print already reflected the concern. The 8-K itself is clean on the primary figures. The transcript is where the behavioral tell appears.

For the margin and housing-watchers, this quarter is not an anomaly to be explained away by weather or timing. It is the first quarter where the company named the deferral out loud in the same breath as the reaffirmed outlook. The guide can stay where it is; the question is whether the consumer the CFO described will cooperate with the back-half assumptions baked into it.

The numbers that moved were the ones that didn't make the beat headlines: transactions, margin, GAAP earnings, and the two words the CFO used to describe what comes next.
